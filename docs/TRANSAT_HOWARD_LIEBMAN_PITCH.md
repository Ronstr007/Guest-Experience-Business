# Transat Pitch for Howard Liebman

## Purpose

Use Howard Liebman as a warm internal bridge into Transat. The goal is not to ask Howard to buy the idea. The goal is to get his reaction, sharpen the positioning, and ask who inside Transat should see it next.

## Strategic positioning

**Core line**

> Airlines have mastered the economics of economy. We help them master the experience of economy.

**Human line**

> The seat may be economy. The experience does not have to be.

**Transat-specific line**

> For a leisure airline, the flight is not just transportation. It is the first scene of the vacation.

## Why this is a good fit for Transat

Transat is a leisure and vacation brand. Its passengers are often travelling for emotionally meaningful reasons: family vacations, weddings, reunions, long-awaited trips, school breaks, anniversaries, first flights and once-a-year escapes.

That gives Transat a natural advantage. A Transat flight can be positioned not only as the way to get to the vacation, but as the beginning of the vacation.

The opportunity is not to make economy expensive or luxurious. It is to make economy and premium economy feel warmer, more human and more memorable through small, operationally realistic moments.

## What to avoid

Do not lead by saying airlines have commoditized their product. That sounds accusatory.

Better framing:

> Airlines have invested billions making travel more efficient. That matters. But the next opportunity is creating moments customers remember and cannot compare on price.

## The offer

A small Transat pilot focused on economy and premium economy guest experience.

The pilot would identify low-cost, high-impact moments across the journey where passengers can feel more welcomed, reassured and emotionally connected to the brand.

The goal is not to redesign the aircraft or burden the crew. The goal is to design moments that fit naturally into the journey and create stories passengers want to repeat.

## One-page pitch memo

### The Experience of Economy: A Guest Experience Opportunity for Transat

Airlines have invested billions making air travel more efficient. That matters. But as schedules, fares and aircraft become increasingly comparable, the customer experience becomes one of the few remaining opportunities for meaningful differentiation.

For Transat, this opportunity is especially powerful.

Transat is not only moving passengers between cities. It is carrying families, couples, students, retirees and vacation travellers toward moments they have often anticipated for months. For many of them, the flight is not simply transportation. It is the first scene of the vacation.

The opportunity is not to make economy luxurious. It is to make economy feel more human.

Airlines have mastered the economics of economy. Transat has an opportunity to master the experience of economy.

A small guest-experience pilot could identify moments across the journey where low-cost, operationally realistic changes create outsized emotional impact. These may include pre-trip communication, check-in, gate language, boarding, family travel, nervous travellers, onboard announcements, small recognition moments, arrival language and the final goodbye.

Ron Shlien brings an unusual background to this challenge. As co-founder of Mad Science and Mad Science Productions, he helped build live educational and theatrical experiences for children and families at scale, including with partners such as LEGO, NASA, theme parks and museums. That work was about turning ordinary moments into memorable experiences through clarity, pacing, surprise, engagement and emotional payoff.

The proposed pilot would use Ron and Shari as dual guest-experience observers. Ron would assess the journey through an experience-design, entrepreneurial and storytelling lens. Shari would assess it through a human, family, hospitality and passenger-comfort lens.

The result would be a practical, executive-ready set of recommendations focused on small moments Transat could test quickly and affordably.

The goal is not to create more operational burden. The goal is to give Transat a distinctive experience signature in the part of the aircraft that matters most: economy and premium economy.

Passengers may buy a ticket because of price. They choose an airline again because of how the journey made them feel.
