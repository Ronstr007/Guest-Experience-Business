# Transat Master Document

## Working title

**The Experience of Economy**

## Signature line

> Airlines have mastered the economics of economy. We help them master the experience of economy.

## One-sentence thesis

Airlines have invested billions making air travel more efficient; the next opportunity is creating memorable, low-cost moments that customers cannot compare on price.

## Executive summary

Air travel has become highly efficient, standardized and price-sensitive. That is not a failure of airline leadership. It is the result of intense competition, consumer price sensitivity, operational complexity and the rise of low-cost carriers.

The opportunity is not to criticize that reality. The opportunity is to build on it.

First class and business class already receive disproportionate design attention. The larger commercial opportunity is economy and premium economy, where most passengers sit and where small moments of warmth, clarity, reassurance and delight can create a disproportionate emotional impact.

This concept proposes a focused guest-experience advisory and pilot program for Air Transat: a practical, low-cost approach to making leisure travel feel more human, memorable and emotionally connected without trying to turn economy into business class.

## Core positioning

Most passengers do not expect luxury. They remember kindness.

The seat may be economy. The experience does not have to be.

Passengers compare prices. They remember experiences.

Airlines already know how to create premium experiences for premium passengers. The bigger opportunity is creating memorable moments for the passengers who make up most of the aircraft.

## Why Transat

Transat is a natural first target because the brand is tied to leisure travel, family travel, vacation anticipation and emotional journeys. Its customers are often not just moving from one city to another; they are beginning honeymoons, family holidays, reunions, long-planned escapes, student trips, retirement travel and once-a-year vacations.

That creates a powerful opening: the flight is not simply transportation. It is the first scene of the trip.

A leisure airline has more permission than many carriers to create moments of warmth, anticipation and story. A Transat flight can feel like the beginning of the vacation, not just the cost of getting there.

## The strategic problem

Airlines tend to compete on:

- price
- schedule
- network
- aircraft
- loyalty benefits
- operational reliability

Those things matter, but they are also easy for customers to compare.

What is harder to compare is how the journey made them feel.

When a customer returns home, the question is not only whether the flight was on time. The stronger question is: what story do they tell?

Do they say:

> The flight was fine.

Or do they say:

> You won't believe what happened on our flight.

The second sentence is where loyalty, brand memory and word-of-mouth live.

## The opportunity

Create a repeatable system of small, operationally realistic moments that make economy and premium economy passengers feel seen, reassured and welcomed.

This is not about luxury. It is about:

- anticipation
- recognition
- warmth
- humor
- clarity
- kindness
- emotional memory
- family comfort
- small surprises
- better storytelling

## What we are not proposing

This is not a proposal to:

- redesign aircraft cabins
- materially increase cost per passenger
- slow down boarding
- add operational complexity
- make economy feel like business class
- create scripted artificial cheerfulness
- burden flight attendants with impractical expectations

## What we are proposing

A practical experience-design pilot that identifies and tests moments across the passenger journey where low-cost interventions can create outsized emotional value.

The goal is not to create a better flight.

The goal is to create a better story.

## Experience design principles

### 1. Make economy feel human again

Passengers know they bought an economy ticket. They do not expect luxury. But they should not feel invisible.

### 2. Focus on moments, not amenities

Amenities cost money. Moments require design.

### 3. Do not add friction

Any intervention must respect boarding, safety, crew workload and operational discipline.

### 4. Build memory around transition points

The best moments happen at emotional transitions: booking, arrival at the airport, boarding, takeoff, meal service, descent, landing and goodbye.

### 5. Create stories customers want to repeat

The experience should produce shareable lines, moments and memories.

## Passenger journey opportunity map

### Before travel

Potential moments:

- a warmer pre-trip message that frames the flight as the beginning of the vacation
- destination-specific anticipation notes
- family travel reassurance
- simple, friendly preparation prompts
- celebratory recognition for honeymoons, birthdays, graduations or first big trips

### Airport and check-in

Potential moments:

- clearer emotional language around the start of the trip
- small recognition moments for families and first-time travellers
- visual cues that make the Transat experience feel more like vacation than processing

### Gate

Potential moments:

- a brief pre-boarding welcome that creates anticipation
- destination-specific fun facts or family prompts
- small surprise moments for children
- better language during delays that combines clarity with reassurance

### Boarding

Potential moments:

- a more intentional welcome line
- crew recognition of children, nervous travellers or milestone travellers
- simple ways to make the aircraft doorway feel less transactional

### Onboard

Potential moments:

- destination-based micro-announcements
- child-friendly moments
- family photo prompts
- kindness moments from crew
- recognition of special trips
- low-cost surprise-and-delight gestures
- better emotional pacing during longer flights

### Arrival

Potential moments:

- a farewell that feels like arrival into vacation
- a final emotional signature line
- recognition that the trip has begun
- a reminder of the return journey as part of the relationship

## Example concepts

### The Vacation Begins Here

A simple boarding and announcement framework that shifts the emotional frame from transportation to vacation.

### Little Moments for Little Travellers

A set of low-cost family-friendly moments for children: a welcome phrase, a mini certificate, a destination clue, a captain-style imagination prompt, or a small activity idea.

### The Story Seat

A rotating micro-moment where selected passengers celebrating a milestone receive a warm acknowledgment that does not invade privacy or slow service.

### Calm in the Cabin

A reassurance framework for nervous travellers: clearer language, warm tone, and simple moments of calm at boarding and takeoff.

### The Transat Goodbye

A memorable arrival/farewell script that gives passengers a positive final emotional cue.

## Pilot proposal

### Phase 1: Discovery

- observe the passenger journey
- interview crew, gate staff and service leaders
- identify emotional friction points
- map moments that are high-impact and low-cost
- review existing customer-experience language

### Phase 2: Moment design

- create 10 to 15 candidate experience moments
- categorize by cost, operational complexity and emotional impact
- design scripts, prompts and staff guidance
- identify moments suitable for economy and premium economy

### Phase 3: Live pilot

- test selected moments on a limited number of flights or routes
- focus on leisure-heavy routes and family travel
- gather passenger and crew feedback
- measure operational impact
- refine the experience kit

### Phase 4: Scale playbook

- produce a repeatable Transat Experience of Economy playbook
- create crew-ready scripts and guidance
- define training modules
- build measurement dashboard
- recommend rollout sequence

For the business, the model can evolve through:

- paid discovery project

- implementation retainer
- training and playbook development
- route-specific experience packages
- annual experience refresh
- licensing of experience modules
- expansion to other leisure airlines, airports, cruise lines and tourism operators
