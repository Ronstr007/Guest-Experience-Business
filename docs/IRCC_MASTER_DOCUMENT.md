# IRCC Master Document

## Working title

**The New Canadian Welcome Experience**

## Signature line

> Becoming Canadian should feel like a milestone, not an appointment.

## One-sentence thesis

Canada should separate the administrative process of citizenship from the emotional ceremony of becoming Canadian, so the final moment feels more like a national welcome and less like clerical processing.

## Executive summary

The citizenship ceremony is one of the most meaningful public-facing moments the Government of Canada delivers. It is the moment when a person who has often waited years, completed requirements, built a life and made a commitment to Canada is formally welcomed as a Canadian citizen.

That moment should feel dignified, warm, memorable and worthy of the milestone.

The current experience often has two competing purposes: administrative completion and ceremonial celebration. Both are necessary, but they do not need to feel like the same experience.

The central idea is simple: separate the clerical work from the ceremony itself.

The administrative work should be clear, efficient and reliable. The ceremony should focus on meaning, belonging, welcome, pride, family and celebration.

This concept proposes a practical redesign of the citizenship ceremony experience for IRCC: not a legal redesign of citizenship, not a partisan initiative, and not an expensive production. It is an experience-design initiative that makes one of Canada's most important civic moments feel as meaningful as it truly is.

## Core positioning

A citizenship ceremony is not just the end of a process. It is the beginning of a story.

New Canadians should not leave feeling processed. They should leave feeling welcomed.

Canada already grants citizenship. The opportunity is to make people feel the weight, warmth and pride of becoming Canadian.

## Why this matters

Citizenship is not an ordinary government service. It is one of the few moments where the state directly welcomes people into the national family.

That creates a rare opportunity:

- to strengthen belonging
- to deepen attachment to Canada
- to affirm the dignity of new citizens
- to include families in the milestone
- to make the ceremony emotionally memorable
- to build civic pride
- to show Canada at its best

For many new citizens, this may be one of the most meaningful interactions they ever have with the Government of Canada.

## The strategic problem

The current ceremony experience can feel overly process-driven because administrative, technical and ceremonial functions are blended together.

This risks making a historic personal milestone feel like:

- a queue
- a compliance step
- a video call
- a checklist
- a document handoff
- an appointment

The administrative work matters. But when it dominates the emotional arc, the ceremony loses power.

## The key insight

Process and ceremony are not the same thing.

Process should be efficient.

Ceremony should be memorable.

When the two are mixed together, the experience becomes neither as efficient nor as meaningful as it could be.

## The proposed solution

Create a redesigned ceremony journey with two distinct tracks:

### 1. The administrative track

Purpose: completion, verification, reliability, clarity.

This should include:

- pre-ceremony instructions
- identity and attendance checks
- technology checks for virtual ceremonies
- document completion
- oath form guidance
- certificate logistics
- troubleshooting
- post-ceremony next steps

Tone: clear, calm, efficient, respectful.

### 2. The ceremonial track

Purpose: meaning, welcome, belonging, pride, celebration.

This should include:

- a stronger opening emotional frame
- a dignified and warm welcome
- clearer explanation of the significance of the moment
- family recognition
- bilingual national tone
- Indigenous recognition and constitutional context handled respectfully
- oath delivery with solemnity and clarity
- celebratory transition after the oath
- a final message that feels like a true welcome to Canada

Tone: warm, dignified, inclusive, celebratory, national.

## Desired emotional outcome

New citizens should feel:

- seen
- welcomed
- proud
- included
- respected
- moved
- connected to Canada
- part of something larger than themselves

Families should feel:

- included in the milestone
- proud of the journey
- able to celebrate
- invited into the memory

IRCC should feel:

- confident the ceremony is legally sound
- confident the process is efficient
- proud of the public-facing experience
- able to deliver consistently across formats

## Experience design principles

### 1. Preserve dignity

The ceremony must remain solemn and respectful. The goal is not spectacle. It is meaning.

### 2. Separate process from meaning

Administrative requirements should not disappear, but they should not dominate the ceremonial moment.

### 3. Design for families

Citizenship is often a family achievement, even when only one person takes the oath.

### 4. Make the moment photographable and memorable

People should have a natural moment to capture and share the milestone.

### 5. Respect bilingualism

The ceremony should feel genuinely Canadian in both official languages.

### 6. Keep it scalable

The solution must work across virtual, hybrid and in-person ceremonies.

### 7. Avoid politics

The tone should be national, civic and inclusive, not partisan.

### 8. Honour Indigenous context thoughtfully

The oath now includes recognition of the Aboriginal and treaty rights of First Nations, Inuit and Métis peoples. The ceremony should treat this as meaningful civic context, not a rushed line.

## The redesigned ceremony journey

### Before the ceremony

Goal: remove stress before the emotional moment.

Potential improvements:

- clearer pre-ceremony communication
- a simple "what will happen" guide
- separate technical/administrative check-in
- a warm message that frames the upcoming milestone
- guidance for family participation
- suggested photo moment instructions

### Opening

Goal: establish that this is not a routine appointment.

Potential improvements:

- a stronger ceremonial opening
- more intentional language from the presiding official
- clear acknowledgement of the journey to citizenship
- emphasis that families and communities are part of the moment

### Before the oath

Goal: help participants understand the significance of what they are about to do.

Potential improvements:

- a brief explanation of the oath in plain, human language
- a solemn pause before the oath
- clearer bilingual pacing
- direct instruction delivered warmly, not mechanically

### The oath

Goal: make the oath the emotional center of the ceremony.

Potential improvements:

- better pacing
- stronger camera/room presence
- explicit recognition that this is the moment citizenship is being conferred
- reduced interruptions or administrative language around the oath

### Immediately after the oath

Goal: transform the room from solemnity to celebration.

Potential improvements:

- a clear emotional transition: "You are now Canadian citizens"
- applause/family recognition
- short celebratory pause
- optional photo moment
- invitation to reflect on the milestone

### Closing

Goal: send new citizens away with pride and belonging.

Potential improvements:

- a closing message that feels personal and national
- reminder of rights and responsibilities
- encouragement to participate in Canadian civic life
- a stronger final welcome
- anthem handled as a moment of unity, not a procedural ending

### After the ceremony

Goal: extend the emotional value beyond the event.

Potential improvements:

- post-ceremony welcome message
- digital keepsake or certificate guide
- "first week as a Canadian" checklist
- suggestions for civic participation
- photo/share moment guidance

## Example ceremony language

The redesign must:

- comply with IRCC requirements
- preserve the legal validity of the oath
- respect bilingual obligations

- be scalable across regions
- support, not burden, IRCC staff
- avoid unnecessary cost
